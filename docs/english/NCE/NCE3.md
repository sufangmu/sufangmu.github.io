## 02 Thirteen Equals One

Looking at his watch, he saw that it was one o'clock, but the bell struck thirteen times before it stopped. <u>Armed with a torch</u>(手电筒), the vicar went up into the clock tower to see <u>what was going on</u>. In the torchlight, he <u>caught sight of a figure</u>(人影) whom he immediately recognized as Bill Wilkins, our local grocer(杂货商).

'I'm trying to repair the bell, ' answered Bill.'I've been coming up here night after night for weeks now.You see, I <u>was hoping</u> to give you a surprise.'

'You certainly did give me a surprise!' said the vicar. 'You've probably woken up everyone in the village <u>as well</u>. Still, <u>I'm glad</u> the bell is working again.'

'That's the trouble, vicar, 'answered Bill. 'It's working all right, but I'm afraid that at one o'clock it will strike thirteen times and <u>there's nothing I can do about it</u>.'

'We'll get used to that, Bill, 'said the vicar. 'Thirteen is not as good as one, but it's better than nothing. Now let's go downstairs and have a cup of tea.'

## 08 A Famous Monastery

The only regular visitors to the monastery in winter are parties of skiers(滑雪的人) who go there at Christmas and Easter(复活节). These young people, who love the peace(平静) of the mountains, always receive a warm welcome at St.Bernard's monastery.

## 25 The Cutty Sark

## 42 Modern Cavemen

The deepest known cave in the world is the Gouffre Berger near Grenoble. It extends to a depth of 3, 723 feet. ## 43 Fully Insured

Insurance(保险) companies are normally willing to insure anything. Insuring public or private property is a standard practice in most countries in the world. Dancing proved to be more than the dish could bear, for during the party it capsized(翻) and sank in seven feet of water.

They had little difficulty in finding it, but hauling(拖拽) it out of the water proved to be a serious problem. Eventually chains were fixed to one end of the dish and a powerful winch(绞车) was put into operation. The dish rose to the surface and was gently drawn towards the canal bank. For one agonizing(精神紧张的) moment, the dish was perched(处于) precariously(危险地) on the bank of the canal, but it suddenly overbalanced and slid back into the water. The dish now had to be lifted vertically(垂直的) because one edge was resting against the side of the canal. The winch was again put into operation and one of the men started up the truck. Several minutes later, the dish was successfully hauled above the surface of the water. Water streamed in torrents(激流) over its sides with such force that it set up a huge wave in the canal. There was a danger that the wave would rebound(弹回) off the other side of the bank and send the dish plunging into the water again. By working at tremendous speed, the men managed to get the dish on to dry land before the wave returned.

## 44 Speed and Comfort

People travelling long distances frequently have to decide whether they would prefer to go by land, sea, or air. Hardly anyone can positively(完全地) enjoy sitting in a train for more than a few hours. Train compartments soon get cramped(窄小的) and stuffy(憋气的). It is almost impossible to take your mind off the journey. Reading is only a partial solution, for the monotonous(枯燥的) rhythm(有节奏的运动) of the wheels clicking(发出咔咔声) on the rails soon lull(催人欲睡) you to sleep. During the day, sleep comes in snatches(短时). At night, when you really wish to go to sleep, you rarely manage to do so. If you are lucky enough to get a sleeper(卧铺), you spend half the night staring at the small blue light in the ceiling, or fumbling(乱摸) to find your ticket for inspection(检查). Inevitably(必然地) you arrive at your destination almost exhausted(精疲力尽). Long car journeys are even less pleasant, for it is quite impossible even to read. On motorways(快车道) you can, at least, travel fairly safely at high speeds, but more often than not, the greater part of the journey is spent on roads with few service stations and too much traffic. By comparison, ferry(渡船) trips or cruises(巡游船) offer a great variety of civilized comforts. You can stretch your legs on the spacious(宽敞的) decks, play games, meet interesting people and enjoy good food--always assuming, of course, that the sea is calm. If it is not, and you are likely to get seasick(晕船), no form of transport could be worse. Even if you travel in ideal weather, sea journeys take a long time. Relatively few people are prepared to sacrifice holiday time for the pleasure of travelling by sea.

Aeroplanes have the reputation of being dangerous and even hardened travellers are intimidated(恐吓) by them. They also have the disadvantage(缺点) of being an expensive form of transport. But nothing can match them for speed and comfort. Travelling at a height of 30, 000 feet, far above the clouds, and at over 500 miles an hour is an exhilarating(令人兴奋的) experience. You do not have to devise ways of taking your mind off the journey, for an aeroplane gets you to your destination rapidly. For a few hours, you settle back in a deep armchair to enjoy the flight. The real escapist(逍遥者) can watch a film and sip(啜) champagne(香槟) on some services. But even when such refinements(精心的安排) are not available, there is plenty to keep you occupied. An aeroplane offers you an unusual and breathtaking(激动人心的) view of the world. You soar(翱翔) effortlessly(不费力的) over high mountains and deep valleys. You really see the shape of the land. If the landscape(景色) is hidden from view, you can enjoy the extraordinary sight of unbroken cloud plains that stretch out for miles before you, while the sun shines brilliantly in a clear sky. The journey is so smooth that there is nothing to prevent you from reading or sleeping. However you decide to spend your time, one thing is certain: you will arrive at your destination fresh(精神饱满的) and uncrumpled(没有垮下来). You will not have to spend the next few days recovering from a long and arduous journey.

## 45 The Power of the Press

In democratic(民主的) countries any efforts to restrict(限制) the freedom of the press are rightly condemned. However, this freedom can easily be abused(滥用). Stories about people often attract far more public attention than political events. Though we may enjoy reading about the lives of others, it is extremely doubtful whether we would equally enjoy reading about ourselves. Acting on the contention(论点) that facts are sacred, reporters can cause untold(无限的) suffering to individuals by publishing details about their private lives. Newspapers exert such tremendous influence that they can not only bring about major changes to the lives of ordinary people but can even overthrow a government.

The story of a poor family that acquired fame and fortune overnight, dramatically illustrates the power of the press. The family lived in Aberdeen, a small town of 23, 000 inhabitants in South Dakota. As the parents had five children, life was a perpetual(永久的) struggle against poverty. They were expecting their sixth child and were faced with even more pressing economic problems. If they had only had one more child, the fact would have passed unnoticed. They would have continued to struggle against economic odds and would have lived in obscurity. But they suddenly became the parents of quintuplets(五胞胎之一), four girls and a boy, an event which radically(彻底地) changed their lives. The day after the birth of the five children, an aeroplane arrived in Aberdeen bringing sixty reporters and photographers.

The rise to fame was swift. Television cameras and newspapers carried the news to everyone in the country. Newspapers and magazines offered the family huge sums for the exclusive rights to publish stories and photographs. Gifts poured in not only from unknown people, but from baby food and soap manufacturers who wished to advertise their products. The old farmhouse the family lived in was to be replaced by a new $500, 000 home. Reporters kept pressing for interviews so lawyers had to be employed to act as spokesmen for the family at press conferences. While the five babies were still quietly sleeping in oxygen tents in a hospital nursery(育婴室), their parents were paying the price for fame. It would never again be possible for them to lead normal lives. They had become the victims of commercialization(商品化), for their names had acquired a market value. Instead of being five new family members, these children had immediately become a commodity(商品).

## 46 Do It Yourself

I gave up in despair. The weeks passed and the grass grew. When my wife nagged(唠叨) me to do something about it, I told her that either I would have to buy a new mower or let the grass grow. Needless to say our house is now surrounded by a jungle. Buried somewhere in deep grass there is a rusting(生锈) lawn mower which I have promised to repair one day.

## 47 Too High a Price

Pollution(污染) is the price we pay for an overpopulated(人口多的), overindustrialized(过度工业化) planet. When you come to think about it, there are only four ways you can deal with rubbish: dump it, burn it, turn it into something you can use again, attempt to produce less of it. We keep trying all four methods, but sheer(纯粹的) volume of rubbish we produce worldwide threatens to overwhelm(淹没) us.

Rubbish, however, is only part of the problem of polluting(污染) our planet. The need to produce ever increasing quantities of cheap food leads to a different kind of pollution. Industriallized farming metheods produce cheap meat products: beef, pork and chicken. The use of pesticides(杀虫剂) and fertilizers(肥料) produces cheap grain and vegetables. The price we pay for cheap food may be already too high: Mad Cow Disease (BSE) in cattle, salmonella(沙门氏菌) in chicken and eggs, and listeria(李斯特杆菌) in dairy products. And if you think you'll abandon meat and become a vegetarian, you have the choice of very expensive organically-grown(有机的) vegetables or a steady diet of pesticides every time you think you're eating fresh salads and vegetables, or just having an innocent glass of water!

However, there is an even more insidious(暗中为害的) kind of pollution that particularly affects urban(城市的) area and invades our daily lives, and that is noise. Burglar(窃贼) alarms going off at any time of the day or night serve only to annoy passers-by and actually assist burglars to burgle. Car alarms constantly scream(尖叫) at us in the street and are a source of profound(极度的) irritation(烦躁). A recent survey of the effects of noise revealed (surprisingly?) that dogs barking incessantly(连续不断地) in the night rated the highest form of noise pollution on a scale ranging from 1 to 7. The survey revealed a large number of sources of noise that we really dislike. Lawn mowers whining(呜呜声) on a summer's day, late-night parties in apartment blocks, noisy neighbours, vehicles of all kinds, especially large container trucks thundering through quiet villages, planes and helicopters(直升机) flying overhead, large radios carried round in public places and played at maximum(最大地) volume. New technology has also made its own contribution to noise. A lot of people object to mobile phones, especially when they are used in public places like restaurant or on public transport. Loud conversations on mobile phones invade our thoughts or interrupt the pleasure of meeting friends for a quiet chat. The noise pollution survey revealed a rather surprising and possibly amusing old-fashioned source of noise. It turned out to be snoring! Men were found to be the worst offenders. It was revealed that 20% of men in their mid-thirties snore(打鼾). This figure rises to a staggering(令人惊愕的) 60% of men in their sixties. Against these figures, it was found that only 5% of women snore regularly, while the rest are constantly woken or kept awake by their trumpeting(吹号) partners. Whatever the source of noise, one thing is certain: silence, it seems, has become a golden memory.

## 48 The Silent Village


In this much-travelled world, there are still thousands of places which are inaccessible(难接近地) to tourists. We always assume that villagers in remote places are friendly and hospitable(好客的). But people who are cut off not only from foreign tourists, but even from their own countrymen can be hostile to travellers. Visits to really remote villages are seldom enjoyable--as my wife and I discovered during a tour through the Balkans.

We had spent several days in a small town and visited a number of old churches in the vicinity(周围). These attracted many visitors, for they were not only of great architectural(建筑) interest, but contained a large number of beautifully preserved frescoes(壁画) as well. On the day before our departure, several bus loads of tourists descended on the town. This was more than we could bear, so we decided to spend our last day exploring the countryside. Taking a path which led out of the town, we crossed a few fields until we came to a dense wood. We expected the path to end abruptly(突然地), but we found that it traced its way through the trees. We tramped(徒步前进) through the wood for over two hours until we arrived at a deep stream. We could see that the path continued on the other side, but we had no idea how we could get across the stream. Suddenly my wife spotted a boat moored(系) to the bank. In it there was a boatman fast asleep. We gently woke him up and asked him to ferry(运) us to the other side. Though he was reluctant to do so at first, we eventually persuaded him to take us.

The path led to a tiny village perched on the steep sides of a mountain. The place consisted of a straggling(蔓延) unmade road which was lined on either side by small houses. Even under a clear blue sky, the village looked forbidding, as all the houses were built of grey mud bricks. The village seemed deserted, the only sign of life being an ugly-looking black goat on a short length of rope tied to a tree in a field nearby. Sitting down on a dilapidated(破烂的) wooden fence near the field, we opened a couple of tins of sardines(沙丁鱼) and had a picnic lunch. All at once, I noticed that my wife seemed to be filled with alarm. Looking up I saw that we were surrounded by children in rags(破烂衣服) who were looking at us silently as we ate. We offered them food and spoke to them kindly, but they remained motionless(不动的). I concluded that they were simply shy of strangers. When we later walked down the main street of the village, we were followed by a silent procession(队伍) of children. The village which had seemed deserted, immediately came to life. Faces appeared at windows. Men in shirt sleeves stood outside their houses and glared at us. Old women in black shawls(围巾) <u>peered(凝视) at</u> us from doorways. The most frightening thing of all was that not a sound could be heard. There was no doubt that we were unwelcome visitors. We needed no further warning. Turning back down the main street, we quickened our pace and made our way rapidly towards the stream where we hoped the boatman was waiting.

## 49 The Ideal Servant


It is a good thing my aunt Harriet died years ago. If she were alive today she would not be able to <u>air her views on</u> her favourite topic of conversation: <u>domestic servants</u>(仆人). Aunt Harriet presided(指挥) over an invisible(看不见地) army of servants that continuously scrubbed(擦拭), cleaned, and polished. Though my aunt pursued(奉行) what was, in those days, an enlightened(开明的) policy, <u>in that</u> she never allowed her domestic staff to work more than eight hours a day, she was extremely difficult to please. Not only was the meal well below the usual standard, but Bessie seemed unable to walk steadily. She bumped into the furniture and kept mumbling about the guests. When she came in with the last course(一道菜)--a huge pudding--she tripped on the carpet(地毯) and the pudding went flying through the air, narrowly missed my aunt, and crashed on the dining table with considerable force. Though this caused great mirth(欢笑) among the guests, Aunt Harriet was horrified. She reluctantly(不情愿地) <u>came to the conclusion that</u> Bessie was drunk. The guests had, of course, realized this from the moment Bessie opened the door for them and, long before the final catastrophe(灾祸), had had a difficult time trying to conceal their amusement. The poor girl was dismissed instantly. After her departure, Aunt Harriet discovered that there were piles of empty wine bottles of all shapes and sizes neatly stacked(整齐堆放) in what had once been Bessie's wardrobe(衣柜). They had mysteriously found their way there from the wine cellar(地窖)!

## 50 New Year Resolutions

## 51 Predicting the Future

​	Predicting the future is notoriously(众所周知地) difficult. Who could have imagined, in the mid 1970s, for example, that by the end of the 20th century, computers would be as common in people's homes as TV sets? In the 1970s, computers were common enough, but only in big business, government departments and large organizations. These were the so-called mainframe(主机) machines. Mainframe computers were very large indeed often occupying whole air-conditioned rooms, employing full-time(专职的) technicians(技师) and run on specially-written software(软件). Though these large machines still exist, many of their functions have been <u>taken over by</u> small powerful personal computers, <u>commonly known as</u> PCs. In 1975, a primitive machine called the Altair, was launched in the USA. It can properly be described as the first 'home computer' and it <u>pointed the way to</u> the future. This was followed, at the end of the 1970s, by a machine called an Apple. In the early 1980s, the computer giant, IBM(美国国际商用机器公司) produced the world's first Personal Computer. This ran on an 'operating system' called DOS(磁盘操作系统), produced by a then small company named Microsoft(微软公司). The IBM Personal Computer was widely copied. From those humble beginnings, we have seen the development of the user-friendly(好用的) home computers and multimedia(多媒体的) machines which are <u>in common use</u> today.

​	Considering how recent these developments are, it is even more remarkable that <u>as long ago as</u> the 1960s, an Englishman, Leon Bagrit, was able to predict some of the uses of computers which we know today. Bagrit dismissed the idea that computers would learn to 'think' for themselves and would 'rule the world', which people liked to believe in those days. Bagrit foresaw a time when computers would be small enough to hold in the hand, when they would <u>be capable of</u> providing information about traffic jams and suggesting alternative(替代的) routes, when they would be used in hospitals to help doctors to diagnose(诊断) illnesses, when they would relieve(减轻) office workers and accountants(会计) of dull, repetitive(重复的) clerical(文书工作的) work. All these computer uses have become commonplace. Of course, Leon Bagrit couldn't possibly have foreseen the development of the Internet(互联网), the worldwide system that <u>enables us to</u> communicate instantly with anyone in any part of the world by using computers <u>linked to</u> telephone networks(网络). Nor could he have foreseen how we could use the Internet to obtain information on every known subject, so we can read it on a screen in our homes and even print it as well if we want to. Computers have become smaller and smaller, more and more powerful and cheaper and cheaper. This is what makes Leon Bagrit's predictions particularly remarkable. If he, or someone like him, were alive today, he might be able to tell us what to expect in the next fifty years.

## 52 Mud is Mud

​	My cousin, Harry, keeps a large curiously-shaped bottle <u>on permanent(永久的) display</u> in his study. Despite the fact that the bottle is tinted(给……染色) a delicate(淡色的) shade(色度) of green, an observant(观察力敏锐的) visitor would soon notice that it is filled with what looks like a thick, greyish(浅灰色的) substance. If you were to ask Harry what was in the bottle, he would tell you that it contained perfumed mud. If you expressed doubt or surprise, he would immediately invite you to smell it and then to rub some into your skin. The brief experiment would dispel(驱散) any further doubts she might have. The bottle really does contain perfumed mud. How Harry <u>came into the possession of</u> this outlandish(稀奇古怪的) stuff makes an interesting story which he is <u>fond of relating</u>. Furthermore, the acquisition(获得) of this bottle cured him of a bad habit he had been developing for years.

​	Harry used to consider it a great joke to go into expensive cosmetic(化妆品) shops and make outrageous(无理的) requests for goods that do not exist. He would invent fanciful(想像出来的) names <u>on the spot</u>. On entering a shop, he would ask for a new perfume called 'Scented Shadow' or for 'insoluble(不可溶解的) bath cubes'. If a shop assistant told him she had not heard of it, he would pretend to be considerably <u>put out</u>. He loved to be told that one of his imaginary products was temporarily <u>out of stock</u> and he would faithfully promise to call again at some future date, but of course he never did. How Harry managed to <u>keep a straight face</u> during these performances is quite beyond me.

​	Harry does not need to be prompted(敦促) to explain how he bought his precious bottle of mud. One day, he went to an exclusive(专售高档商品的) shop in London and asked for 'Myrolite'. The shop assistant looked puzzled and Harry repeated the word, slowly stressing each syllable(音节). When the woman shook her head in bewilderment(迷惑), Harry went on to explain that 'myrolite' was a hard, amber-like substance which could be used to remove freckles(雀斑). This explanation evidently(显然地) conveyed something to the woman who searched <u>shelf after shelf</u>. She produced all sorts of weird(奇异的) concoctions(调制品), but none of them <u>met with</u> Harry's requirements. When Harry <u>put on his act</u> of being mildly annoyed, the assistant promised to order some for him. Intoxicated(陶醉) by his success, Harry then asked for perfumed mud. He expected the assistant to look at him in blank(茫然的) astonishment. However, it was his turn to be surprised, for the woman's eyes immediately <u>lit up</u> and she fetched several bottles which she placed on the counter for Harry to inspect. For once, Harry had to <u>admit defeat</u>. He picked up what seemed to be the smallest bottle and discreetly(谨慎地) asked the price. He was glad to <u>get away with</u> a mere twenty pounds and he <u>beat a hasty retreat</u>, clutching(抓住) the precious bottle under his arm. <u>From then on</u>, Harry decided that this little game he had invented might prove to be expensive. The curious bottle which now adorns(装饰) the bookcase in his study was his first and last purchase of rare cosmetics(化妆品).

## 53 In the Public Interest

​	The Scandinavian countries are much admired all over the world for their enlightened social policies. Sweden has evolved(逐渐形成) an excellent system for protecting the individual citizen from high-handed(高压的，专横的) or incompetent(不称职的) public officers. The system has worked so well, that it has been adopted in other countries too.

​	The Swedes were the first to recognize that public officials like civil servants, police officers, health inspectors or tax-collectors can make mistakes or act over-zealously(过分热情地) <u>in the belief that</u> they are serving the public. As long ago as 1809, the Swedish Parliament introduced a scheme to safeguard(保护) the interest of the individual. A parliamentary(国会的) committee representing all political parties appoints a person who is suitably qualified(合格的) to investigate private grievances(不平，冤屈) against the State. The official title of the person is 'Justiteombudsman', but the Swedes commonly <u>refer to</u> him <u>as</u> the 'J.O.' or 'Ombudsman'. The Ombudsman is not <u>subject to</u> political pressure. He investigates complaints large and small that come to him from all levels of society. As complaints must be made in writing, the Ombudsman receives an average of 1,200 letters a year. He has eight lawyer assistants to help him and he examines every single letter <u>in detail</u>. There is nothing secretive(保密的) about the Ombudsman's work, for his correspondence(来往信件) is <u>open to</u> public inspection. If a citizen's complaint is justified the Ombudsman will act <u>on his behalf</u>. The action he takes varies according to the nature of the complaint. He may gently reprimand an official or even suggest to parliament that a law be altered(改变). The following case is a typical example of the Ombudsman's work.

​	A foreigner living in a Swedish village wrote to the Ombudsman complaining that he had been ill-treated by the police, simply because he was a foreigner. The Ombudsman immediately wrote to the Chief of Police in the district asking him to send a record of the case. There was nothing in the record to show that the foreigner's complaint was justified and the Chief of Police strongly denied the accusation(谴责，指控). It was impossible for the Ombudsman to <u>take action</u>, but when he received a similar complaint from another foreigner in the same village, he immediately sent one of his lawyers to investigate the matter. The lawyer ascertained(查明) that a policeman had indeed dealt roughly with foreigners on several occasions. The fact that the policeman was prejudiced(有偏见的) against foreigners could not be recorded in the official files. It was only possible for the Ombudsman to find this out by sending one of his representatives to check the facts. The policeman <u>in question</u> was severely reprimanded and was informed that if any further complaints were <u>lodged against</u> him, he would be prosecuted. The Ombudsman's prompt(即时的，迅速的) action at once <u>put an end to</u> an unpleasant practice which might have <u>gone unnoticed</u>.

## 54 Instinct or Cleverness

​	We have <u>been brought up to</u> fear insects. We <u>regard them as</u> unnecessary creatures that do more harm than good. We continually <u>wage war on</u> them, for they contaminate(弄脏) our food, carry diseases, or devour(吞食) our crops. They sting or bite without provocation(惹怒); they fly uninvited into our rooms on summer nights, or beat against our lighted windows. We live <u>in dread of</u> not only of unpleasant insects like spiders(蜘蛛) or wasps(黄蜂), but of quite harmless ones like moths(飞蛾). Reading about them increases our understanding without dispelling our fears. Knowing that the industrious(勤勉的) ant(蚂蚁) lives in a highly organized society does nothing to prevent us from being filled with revulsion(厌恶) when we find hordes(群) of them crawling over a carefully prepared picnic lunch. No matter how much we like honey, or how much we have read about the uncanny(神秘的，不可思议的) sense of direction which bees possess, we have a horror of being stung. Most of our fears are unreasonable, but they are impossible to erase(擦，抹去). At the same time, however, insects are strangely fascinating. We enjoy reading about them, especially when we find that, like the praying mantis(螳螂), they lead perfectly horrible lives. We enjoy staring at them, entranced(出神的) as they go about their business, unaware (we hope) of our presence. Who has not <u>stood in awe</u> at the sight of a spider pouncing on a fly, or a column of ants triumphantly bearing home an enormous dead beetle(甲虫)?

​	Last summer I spent days in the garden watching thousands of ants crawling up the trunk of my prize peach tree. The tree has grown against a warm wall on a sheltered(无危险的) side of the house. I am especially proud of it, not only because it has survived several severe winters, but because it occasionally produces luscious(甘美的) peaches. During the summer, I noticed that the leaves of the tree were beginning to wither(枯萎). Clusters(一簇) of tiny insects called aphids(蚜虫) were to be found on the underside(底面，下侧) of the leaves. They were visited by a large colony(一群) of ants which obtained a sort of honey from them. I immediately <u>embarked on</u> an experiment which even though it failed to <u>get rid of</u> the ants kept me fascinated for twenty-four hours. I bound the base of the tree with sticky(粘的) tape, making it impossible for the ants to reach the aphids. The tape was so sticky that they did not dare to cross it. For a long time, I watched them scurrying(小步跑) around the base of the tree <u>in bewilderment</u>. I even went out at midnight with a torch and noted with satisfaction (and surprise) that the ants were still swarming(聚集) around the sticky tape without being able to do anything about it. I got up early next morning hoping to find that the ants had <u>given up in despair</u>. Instead, I saw that they had discovered a new route. They were climbing up the wall of the house and then on to the leaves of the tree. I realized sadly that I had been completely defeated by their ingenuity(机灵). The ants had been quick to find an answer to my thoroughly unscientific methods!

## 55 From the Earth: Greetings

​	Recent developments in astronomy(天文学) have made it possible to detect planets in our own Milky Way and in other galaxies(星系). This is a major achievement because, <u>in relative terms</u>, planets are very small and do not emit(散发) light. Finding planets is proving hard enough, but finding life on them will prove infinitely(无限地) more difficult. The first question to answer is whether a planet can actually support life. In our own solar(太阳系的) system, for example, Venus(金星) is far too hot and Mars(火星) is far too cold to support life. Only the Earth provides ideal conditions, and even here it has taken more than four billion years for plant and animal life to evolve(进化).

​	Whether a planet can support life depends on the size and brightness of its star, that is its 'sun'. Imagine a star up to twenty times larger, brighter and hotter than our own sun. A planet would have to be a very long way from it to <u>be capable of</u> supporting life. Alternatively, if the star were small, the life-supporting planet would have to have a close orbit(运行轨道) round it and also provide the perfect conditions for life forms to develop. But how would we find such a planet? At present, there is no telescope <u>in existence</u> that is capable of detecting the presence of life. The development of such a telescope will be one of the great astronomical(天文学的) projects of the 21st century.

​	It is impossible to look for life on another planet using earth-based telescopes. Our own warm atmosphere and the heat generated(产生) by the telescope would make it impossible to detect objects as small as planets. Even a telescope in orbit round the earth, like the very successful Hubble telescope, would not be suitable because of the dust particles(微粒，粒子) in our solar system. A telescope would have to be as far away as the planet Jupiter(木星) to look for life in outer space, because the dust becomes thinner the further we travel towards the outer edges of our own solar system. Once we detected a planet, we would have to find a way of <u>blotting out</u> the light from its star, so that we would be able to 'see' the planet properly and analyse(分析) its atmosphere. <u>In the first instance</u>, we would be looking for plant life, <u>rather than</u> 'little green men'. The life forms most likely to develop on a planet would be bacteria(细菌). It is bacteria that have generated the oxygen(氧气) we breathe on earth. For most of the earth's history they have been the only form of life on our planet. As Earth-dwellers(居民), we always cherish the hope that we will be visited by little green men and that we will be able to communicate with them. But this hope is always in the realms(领域) of science fiction. If we were able to discover lowly(低微的) forms of life like bacteria on another planet, it would completely change our view of ourselves. As Daniel Goldin of NASA observed, 'Finding life elsewhere would change everything. No human endeavour(努力) or thought would be unchanged by it.'

## 56 Our Neighbour, the River

​	The river which forms the eastern boundary(界线，边界) of our farm has always <u>played an important part in</u> our lives. Without it we could not <u>make a living</u>. There is only enough spring water to supply the needs of the house so we have to pump(用泵抽) from the river for farm use. We tell the river all our secrets. We know instinctively, just as beekeepers with their bees, that misfortune might overtake(突然降临) us if the important events of our lives were not related to it.

​	We have special river birthday parties in the summer. Sometimes we go upstream to a favourite backwater(回水河汊), sometimes we have our party at the boathouse, which a predecessor(前任，前辈) of ours at the farm built in the meadow(草地，草场) hard by the deepest pool for swimming and diving. In a heat wave we choose a midnight birthday party and that is the most exciting of all. We welcome the seasons by the riverside, crowning(给……戴花环) the youngest girl with flowers in the spring, holding a summer festival on Midsummer Eve, giving thanks for the harvest in the autumn, and throwing a holly(冬青植物) wreath(花环) into the current in the winter.

​	After a long period of rain the river may overflow its banks. This is a rare occurrence(偶发事件) as our climate seldom goes to extremes. We are lucky <u>in that</u> only the lower fields, which <u>make up</u> a very small proportion(部分) of our farm, are affected by flooding, but other farms are less favourably sited(使位于), and flooding can sometimes spell(招致，带来) disaster for their owners.

​	One bad winter we watched the river creep up the lower meadows. All the cattle had been moved into stalls(牲口棚) and we stood to lose little. We were, however, worried about our nearest neighbours, whose farm was low lying and who were newcomers to the district. As the floods had <u>put the telephone out of order</u>(注：put sth. out of order 使……发生故障), we could not find out how they were managing. From an attic(顶楼) window we could get a sweeping(范围广大的) view of the river where their land joined ours, and at the most critical(危急的) juncture(时刻，关头) we <u>took turns in</u> watching that point. The first sign of disaster was a dead sheep floating down. Next came a horse, swimming bravely, but we were afraid that the strength of the current would prevent its landing anywhere before it became exhausted. Suddenly a raft(木筏) appeared, looking rather like Noah's ark, carrying the whole family, a few hens, the dogs, a cat, and a bird in a cage. We realized that they must have become unduly(过度地) frightened by the rising flood, for their house, which had sound foundations(地基), would have stood stoutly(牢固地) even if it had been almost submerged(浸没). The men of our family waded down through our flooded meadows with boat hooks, <u>in the hope of</u> being able to grapple(抓住) a corner of the raft and pull it out of the current towards our bank. We still think it a miracle that they were able to do so.

## 57 Back in the Old Country

​	I stopped to let the car <u>cool off</u> and to study the map. I had expected to be near my objective <u>by now</u>, but everything still seemed alien(异国的，陌生的) to me. I was only five when my father had taken me abroad, and that was eighteen years ago. When my mother had died after a tragic accident, he did not quickly recover from the shock and loneliness. Everything around him was full of her presence, continually reopening the wound. So he decided to emigrate(移居国外). In the new country he <u>became absorbed in</u> making a new life for the two of us, so that he gradually ceased to grieve. He did not marry again and I was <u>brought up</u> without a woman's care; but I lacked for nothing, for he was both father and mother to me. He always meant to go back one day, but not to stay. His roots and mine had become too firmly embedded(扎牢的，嵌入的) in the new land. But he wanted to see the old folk again and to visit my mother's grave. He became mortally(致命地) ill a few months before we had planned to go and, when he knew that he was dying, he made me promise to go <u>on my own</u>.

​	I hired a car the day after landing and bought a comprehensive(全面的，详尽的) book of maps, which I found most helpful on the cross-country journey, but which I did not think I should need on the last stage. It was not that I actually remembered anything at all. But my father had described <u>over and over again</u> what we should see at every milestone(里程碑) after leaving the nearest town, so that I was positive I should recognize it as familiar territory(领地，地区). Well, I had been wrong, for I was now lost.

​	I looked at the map and then at the milometer(计程表). I had come ten miles since leaving the town and at this point, according to my father, I should be looking at farms and cottages in a valley, with the spire(塔尖) of the church of our village showing in the far distance. I could see no valley, no farms, no cottages and no church spire-only a lake. I decided that I must have <u>taken a wrong turning</u> somewhere. So I drove back to the town and began to retrace(返回，重走) the route, taking frequent glances at the map. I <u>landed up</u> at the same corner. The curious thing was that the lake was not marked on the map. I felt as if I had stumbled(趔趄地走) into a nightmare country, as you sometimes do in dreams. And, as in a nightmare, there was nobody <u>in sight</u> to help me. Fortunately for me, as I was wondering what to do next, there appeared on the horizon(地平线) a man <u>on horseback</u>, riding in my direction. I waited till he came near, then I asked him the way to our old village. He said that there was now no village. I thought he must have misunderstood me, so I repeated its name. This time he <u>pointed to</u> the lake. The village no longer existed because it had been submerged, and all the valley too. The lake was not a natural one, but a man-made reservoir(水库).

## 58 A Spot of Bother

​	The old lady was glad to be back at the <u>block of flats</u> where she lived. Her shopping had tired her and her basket had grown heavier with every step of the way home. In the lift(电梯) her thoughts were on lunch and a good rest; but when she got out at her own floor, both were forgotten in her sudden discovery that her front door was open. She was thinking that she must reprimand her home help the next morning for such a monstrous(极大的，可怕的) piece of negligence(粗心大意), when she remembered that she had gone shopping after the home help had left and she knew that she had turned both keys in their locks. She walked slowly into the hall and at once noticed that all the room doors were open, yet following her <u>regular practice</u> she had shut them before going out. Looking into the drawing room, she saw a scene of confusion over by her writing desk. It was <u>as clear as daylight</u> then that burglars had <u>forced an entry</u> during her absence. Her first impulse was to go round all the rooms looking for the thieves, but then she decided that at her age it might be more prudent(谨慎的) to have someone with her, so she went to fetch the porter from his basement. By this time her legs were beginning to tremble, so she sat down and accepted a cup of very strong tea, while he telephoned the police. Then, her composure(镇静，沉着) regained, she was ready to <u>set off</u> with the porter's assistance to search for any intruders(入侵者) who might still be lurking(潜藏) in her flat.

​	They went through the rooms, being careful to touch nothing, as they did not want to hinder(妨碍) the police in their search for fingerprints(指纹). The chaos(混乱) was inconceivable(不可思议的). She had lived in the flat for thirty years and was a veritable(真正的，地地道道的) magpie(喜欢收藏物品的人) at hoarding: and it seemed as though everything she possessed had been tossed(扔) out and turned over and over. At least <u>sorting out</u> the things she should have discarded(丢弃) years ago was now being made easier for her. Then a police inspector arrived with a constable(警察) and she told them of her discovery of the ransacked(洗劫) flat. The inspector began to look for fingerprints, while the constable checked that the front door locks had not been forced, thereby proving that the burglars had either used <u>skeleton keys</u> or entered over the balcony(阳台). There was no trace of fingerprints, but the inspector found a dirty red bundle that contained jewellery which the old lady said was not hers. So their entry into this flat was apparently not the burglars' first job that day and they must have been disturbed. The inspector then asked the old lady to try to <u>check what was missing</u> by the next day, and advised her not to stay alone in the flat for a few nights. The old lady thought he was a fussy(大惊小怪的) creature, but since the porter agreed with him, she rang up her daughter and asked for her help in what she described as a little <u>spot of bother</u>.

## 59 Collecting

​	People tend to amass(积聚) possessions, sometimes without <u>being aware of</u> doing so. Indeed they can have a delightful surprise when they find something useful which they did not know they owned. Those who never have to move house become indiscriminate(不加选择的) collectors of what can only be described as clutter(一堆杂物). They leave unwanted objects in drawers, cupboards and attics for years, <u>in the belief that</u> they may one day need just those very things. As they grow old, people also accumulate belongings for two other reasons, lack of physical and mental energy, both of which are essential in <u>turning out</u> and <u>throwing away</u>, and sentiment. Things owned for a long time are full of associations with the past, perhaps with relatives who are dead, and so they gradually acquire a value beyond their true worth.

​	Some things are collected deliberately in the home <u>in an attempt to</u> avoid waste. Among these I would list string(细线) and brown paper, kept by thrifty people when a parcel has been opened, to save buying these two requisites(必需品).

​	Collecting small items can easily become a mania(癖好). I know someone who always cuts sketches(图样) out from newspapers of model clothes that she would like to buy if she had the money. As she is not rich, the chances that she will ever be able to afford such purchases are remote(小的); but she is never sufficiently strongminded(意志坚强的) to be able to stop the practice. It is a harmless habit, but it litters up her desk <u>to such an extent that</u> every time she opens it, loose bits of paper fall out in every direction. Collecting as a serious hobby is quite different and has many advantages. It provides relaxation(娱乐) for leisure hours, as just looking at one's treasures is always a joy. One does not have to go outside for amusement, since the collection is housed at home. Whatever it consists of, stamps, records, first editions of books, china, glass, antique furniture, pictures, model cars, stuffed birds, toy animals, there is always something to do <u>in connection with</u> it, from finding the right place for the latest addition, to verifying(查证，核实) facts in reference books. This hobby educates one not only in the chosen subject, but also in general matters which <u>have a bearing on</u> it. There are also other benefits. One wants to meet like-minded(志趣相投的) collectors, to get advice, to <u>compare notes</u>, to exchange articles, to <u>show off</u> the latest find. So one's circle of friends grows. Soon the hobby leads to travel, perhaps to a meeting in another town, possibly a trip abroad <u>in search of</u> a rare specimen(标本), for collectors are not <u>confined to</u> any one country. Over the years, one may well become an authority on one's hobby and will very probably be asked to give informal talks to little gatherings and then, if successful, to larger audiences. In this way self-confidence grows, first from mastering a subject, then from being able to talk about it. Collecting, by occupying spare time so constructively(有益的), makes a person contented(心满意足的), with no time for boredom(无聊).

## 60 Too Early and Too Late

​	Punctuality(准时) is a necessary habit in all public affairs in civilized society. Without it, nothing could ever be <u>brought to a conclusion</u>; everything would be <u>in a state of</u> chaos. Only in a sparsely-populated rural(农村的) community is it possible to disregard(无视) it. In ordinary living, there can be some tolerance of unpunctuality. The intellectual(知识分子), who is working on some abstruse(深奥的) problem, has everything coordinated(协调) and organized for the matter in hand. He is therefore forgiven if late for a dinner party. But people are often reproached(责备) for unpunctuality when their only fault is <u>cutting things fine</u>. It is hard for energetic, quick-minded people to waste time, so they are often tempted to finish a job before <u>setting out</u> to <u>keep an appointment</u>. If no accidents occur on the way, like punctured(刺破) tyres, diversions(绕道) of traffic, sudden descent of fog, they will be <u>on time</u>. They are often more industrious, useful citizens than those who are never late. The over-punctual can be as much a trial(讨厌的事、人) to others as the unpunctual. The guest who arrives half an hour too soon is the greatest nuisance. Some friends of my family had this irritating habit. The only thing to do was ask them to come half an hour later than the other guests. Then they arrived just when we wanted them.

​	If you are catching a train, it is always better to be comfortably early than even <u>a fraction(很小一点儿) of</u> a minute too late. Although being early may mean wasting a little time, this will be less than if you miss the train and have to wait an hour or more for the next one; and you avoid the frustration of arriving at the very moment when the train is <u>drawing out of</u> the station and being unable to get on it. An even harder situation is to be on the platform <u>in good time</u> for a train and still to see it go off without you. Such an experience befell a certain young girl the first time she was travelling alone.

​	She entered the station twenty minutes before the train <u>was due</u>, since her parents had <u>impressed upon</u> her that it would be unforgivable to miss it and cause the friends with whom she was going to stay to make two journeys to meet her. She gave her luggage to a porter and showed him her ticket. <u>To her horror</u> he said that she was two hours too soon. She <u>felt in</u> her handbag for the piece of paper on which her father had written down all the details of the journey and gave it to the porter. He agreed that a train did come into the station at the time on the paper and that it did stop, but only to <u>take on</u> mail, not passengers. The girl asked to see a timetable, feeling sure that her father could not have made such a mistake. The porter went to fetch one and <u>arrived back</u> with the station master, who produced it with a flourish(打手势) and pointed out a microscopic(微小的) 'o' beside the time of the arrival of the train at his station; this little 'o' indicated that the train only stopped for mail. Just as that moment the train came into the station. The girl, tears <u>streaming down</u> her face, begged to be allowed to <u>slip into</u> the <u>guard's van</u>. But the station master was adamant(坚定的): rules could not be broken. And she had to watch that train disappear towards her destination while she was <u>left behind</u>.
